The history of tea leaf in the world

The history of tea in brief According to the stories, tea has been drunk in China for almost 5,000 years. Around 1300–1500, the tea ceremony developed in Japan. Only in the middle of the 16th century did tea reach Europe via merchants from Venice. The tea came to England around 1650 and came to be drunk in court circles in the upper echelons of society and after a while also in the coffee houses of the cities. It quickly became a popular drink. The first tea imported to Europe was green tea from China. During the 19th century, Sri Lankan and Indian teas also began to be imported. However, the fresh green tea was difficult to ship because it often went moldy. Therefore, the process of drying and fermenting/oxidizing the tea was developed.

The English Tea production in the world has been largely controlled and spread by the English. When they were no longer allowed to buy tea in China in the 1830s, they smuggled tea plants to India and Sri Lanka. When these countries became independent after the Second World War and began to nationalize their plantations, the English moved a large part of the production to East Africa. Today, the largest tea producers are China, India, Sri Lanka and Kenya.

The Tea Party That Started a War - Boston Tea Party On May 10, 1773, the British Parliament decided to save the East India Company from bankruptcy by allowing them to export large quantities of tea to the colonies in North America without paying the usual fees such as taxes and duties. Using these advantages, the East India Company was able to gain a monopoly on the tea trade. It was not the first time that the colonies were disadvantaged by the laws and regulations of the British Parliament. This decision was the final straw. On the chilly night of December 16, 1773, what is known today as the "Boston Tea Party" occurred. Under cover of darkness, colonists dressed as Indians crept aboard three of the East India Company's ships anchored in Boston Harbor. There they put tea worth thousands of pounds overboard. An offended British Parliament took strong countermeasures, which was one of the starting points for the American War of Independence (1775–83).
